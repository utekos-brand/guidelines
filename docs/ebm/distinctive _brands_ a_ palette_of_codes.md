# Distinctive Brands Paint With a Palette of Codes

September 26, 2025
By Rachel Morris

## Distinctive brands “paint with a palette of codes,” says Mark Ritson

A distinctive palette of codes drives stronger brand performance. Learn how to build yours, test existing assets, and understand why the right combination of codes is vital to your brand’s long-term success.
Mark Ritson has been on a public crusade for unity within the marketing discipline. He has even reached out to the father of distinctiveness himself, Byron Sharp, to agree on common ground.
In the meantime, however, we must navigate a few synonymous terms. Distinctive Brand Assets (`Ehrenberg-Bass Institute`), Fluency (`System1`), and Brand Codes (`MiniMBA`) all refer to the same concept: how quickly and easily consumers recognise a brand and its assets—logos, sounds, characters, colours, and more.

What everyone agrees on is that brand codes are essential for brand growth. Whether you are a challenger brand or an industry leader, brand codes matter. Brands must work hard to stand apart from competitors, certainly—but first, they must stand out and be distinctive.

“Distinctiveness has got nothing to do with competitors,” Mark says. “It’s all about how I stand forward in your consciousness, so you know that it’s me. It is, as `Byron Sharp` says, how a brand ensures that it looks like itself.”

But where do brand codes actually come from? Coca-Cola’s contour bottle was famously inspired by the kola nut from which it was originally made, while Veuve Clicquot’s now-signature yellow label resulted from a printing mishap. The point is that codes can originate from anywhere.

“Some codes aren’t meaningful,” says Mark. “They’re just elements that became attached to the brand and formed part of the associative network in the consumer’s mind. If the code evokes the brand and makes it distinctive, we’ll take it—as long as it makes the customer notice me.”

If a code makes the brand distinctive, it is valuable—as long as it captures attention.

These visual, aural, and sensory cues are carefully refined and combined into a “palette of codes.” Going forward, these elements become the tools brand managers use to “paint” the brand across every touchpoint, building distinctiveness over time.

Using his consultancy work for Sephora as an example, Mark illustrates what a refined palette of codes looks like. We can recognise the logo, the wave, the black-and-white stripes, and the touch of red.

*“That doesn’t look like much,”* says Mark. *“A huge billion-dollar brand like Sephora has only four codes. What you learn is that three or four—maybe five—codes is really the maximum number you want to create a highly distinctive brand environment. The way you use these symbolic assets can vary enormously.”*

### Choosing the right brand codes

Mark recommends starting with a brand’s history when conducting Brand Diagnosis work. In many cases, the archives are your most valuable resource.

“Whether your brand is 20 years old or 200 years old, you should dig through its history to identify anything that could potentially serve as a brand code.”

### The archives are often your best resource

“This is akin to being dealt codes from the hand of history. You’ve been given elements—sometimes by accident, sometimes deliberately. Products have been designed, ads have run, events have occurred, and associations have formed that customers may recognise.”

For example, in 2020, Asda permanently revived its ‘pocket tap’ brand code, first introduced in 1977. While it may not make logical sense in a contactless world, the gesture is so deeply ingrained in consumers’ minds that the brand reintroduced it as part of a long-term strategy to reclaim its position as the supermarket of value.

You can also look further back for inspiration—from founders (Coco Chanel and minimalist personal style), brand heritage (Barbour and its ties to the British Royal Family), or location (Donna Karan New York).

Start with a long list of potential codes, including those already in use and those uncovered through research. The next step is to refine this list to identify the few codes that offer the greatest potential for distinctiveness.

While the discovery stage may seem somewhat subjective, *“the selection process becomes highly strategic—and critically important to the long-term success of your brand,”* says Mark.

Before conducting qualitative and quantitative research, Mark suggests consulting the creative team. “The first place I turn is to creative people. They are best positioned to assess which codes have the potential to work in the future. Ask them which codes have the most longevity and impact.”

With a refined list of codes, you can then conduct qualitative research. A particularly effective shortcut, according to Mark, is to speak with loyal customers.

### Asking loyalists about the brand is a great way to mainline the brand image

*“Ask loyalists what images, associations, and cues instantly signal the brand’s presence. Loyalists can often recognise your brand immediately—even without the logo—because they are familiar with its codes.”*

For deeper analysis, you can move into quantitative research. Professor `Jenni Romaniuk` has developed widely recognised methods for measuring brand codes, particularly through her fame/uniqueness framework.

*“Jenni Romaniuk’s work is exceptional,”* says Mark. *“In her book, `Building Distinctive Brand Assets`, she explains how to quantitatively measure codes to determine which ones to prioritise.”*

Rather than starting with brand awareness, Romaniuk’s approach begins with the code itself. Researchers assess how many consumers can associate a given code with a specific brand without prompting.

The results are plotted on an X and Y axis representing `fame` and `uniqueness`, forming what Romaniuk calls the `Distinctive Assets Grid`.

### Distinctive Assets Grid, Ehrenberg-Bass Institute

For example, in testing Ferrari, participants might be shown a specific red Pantone and asked which brand comes to mind. If 85 out of 100 respondents mention Ferrari, the code achieves an 85% fame score.

However, if only 5% of respondents exclusively associate that colour with Ferrari, the uniqueness score is 5%. This places Ferrari’s `Rosso Corsa` red in the “avoid solo use” category.

In contrast, Coca-Cola’s contour bottle achieved near-perfect results in 1949, with 99% of respondents associating it exclusively with Coke. This places it firmly in the top-right “use or lose” quadrant.

*“It was, frankly, perfect,”* says Mark. *“We aim to identify codes that fall into this quadrant.”*

### It was, frankly, perfect

The bottom-left quadrant represents codes with low fame and low uniqueness—labelled `ignore or test`. In reality, these codes are often discarded.

Another category includes codes with high uniqueness but low fame. *“Romaniuk calls these ‘investment potential’ codes,”* Mark explains. *“With sufficient investment, they could become valuable assets. However, in practice, we prioritise codes that already demonstrate both fame and uniqueness.”*

While more advanced analytical methods are available, many brands do not need to go that far. The most important step is simply to start using your brand codes consistently.

### The most important thing about brand codes is that you start using them

Repeated application of brand codes across all consumer touchpoints is what enables a brand to become recognisable.

Only after achieving strong recognition can brands begin to unlock the deeper benefits of brand codes.

Ferrari’s red may evoke passion or fearlessness—but not in isolation. Otherwise, we would feel the same excitement when seeing a British post box. Meaning is created through the consistent use of a combined palette of codes, supported by positioning, communications, and broader brand strategy.

Brand codes connect all elements of the brand, helping consumers first notice and then understand the product—ultimately increasing the likelihood of purchase, profitability, and growth.
